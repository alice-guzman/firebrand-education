The Dimensions of Human Sexuality

The Sexuality Information and Education Council of the United States (SIECUS) defines human sexuality as encompassing the sexual knowledge, beliefs, attitudes, values, and behaviors of individuals. We define human sexuality as part of your total personality, a natural part of life. See Link to Asexuality and Autochorissexualism (Identityless Sexuality) Reading .The biological dimension includes basic physiology and genetics (Greenberg, Bruess, & Oswalt, 2014). See Link to Sex and the human skeleton Reading. On the one hand, biological knowledge helps us understand how our bodies work (e.g., physical appearance, sexual stimulation, and reproduction). On the other hand, genetic knowledge helps us understand that much of our sexuality is unconscious; predetermined by our biological nature. The psychological dimension refers to our sense of well-being. This dimension helps form our attitudes and feelings about sexuality. The sociocultural dimension is the sum of cultural and social influences that affect our sexual thoughts and actions (Greenberg et al., 2014).

Historical and Cultural Influences


See Link to Sexuality in "primitive" society Reading

The sexual revolution began around the early part of the 20th century. With the advent of more reliable forms of contraceptives and greater acceptance of sexuality in public forums, the revolution continues into the new millennium (Greenberg et al., 2014). Many of the issues concerning the control of sexual behavior remain the same today as they did at the beginning of human society. See Link to The psychology of human sexuality Reading. Does sexual activity outside of marriage have a purpose other than procreation? Since science's first understanding of the role of conception in the the process of reproduction emerged in the latter part of the 19th century, the issue of contraception and the use of the devices used to prevent or even terminate pregnancy (e.g. condoms, oral contraceptives, and abortion) have been discussed and debated (Greenberg et al., 2014). Thinking Critically About Human Sexuality

See Link to Introduction: The ethics of sex education Reading

It is important to be able to understand and interpret information related to human sexuality utilizing an analytical thought process. In other words, one needs to think critically about human sexuality to prevent oneself from blindly accepting scientific assumptions, arguments, and conclusions. Being a good consumer of sexual information means evaluating research according to its merits. To do so, one needs not only to know what questions to ask, but also to understand some of the terminology associated with sexuality research (Greenberg et al., 2014). For example, a correlation shows a relationship between two events. Additionally, causation suggests a relationship in which one event causes another event to occur.

To effectively use scientific research and follow the protocols of the scientific method, one needs to:

  1. question the assumptions, arguments, and conclusions made

  2. examine the method(s) utilized and the corresponding data

  3. determine some of the reasons why the research may have been published.

These represent some of the principles of the scientific method (Greenberg et al., 2014).

Why Study Human Sexuality?

One studies human sexuality to develop a strong conceptual base; a conceptual base that includes sexual knowledge, personal values, sexual decision making, and sexual health and wellness. See Link to Sexual identity and sexual orientation Reading. For example, accurate sexual knowledge may help one dispel sexual myths, superstitions, and misinformation that impede communication and create confusion. See Link to Sexuality education Reading. A clarification of one's personal values may help in deciding those beliefs that are most important to you. Also, sexual decision making may help one transcend sexual experience and aid in the process of developing the skills and wisdom that are necessary to make good health decisions about sex (e.g. being prepared to practice safe sex). See Link to Attitudes Towards Affectivity and Sexuality of People with Intellectual Disability Reading.Finally, practicing safer sexual behavior can promote sexual health and wellness and improve self-esteem (Greenberg et al., 2014). Conclusion

"Studying human sexuality is important to obtain accurate sexual knowledge, clarify personal values, improve sexual decision making, understand the relationship between human sexuality and personal well-being, and explore how the varied dimensions of human sexuality influence one's sexuality" (Greenberg, et al., 2014, p. 26)

Sexuality involves the interrelationship of biological, psychological, and sociocultural dimensions and is influenced by religion, culture, ethics, and politics. Throughout history there has always been an attempt to control sexual behavior, as evidenced by moral and legal codes of the time. There have also been several theories and myths about conception. It was 1875 before there was even rudimentary understanding of the male's sperm penetrating and combining with the female's egg to produce offspring (Greenberg, Bruess, & Oswalt, 2014).
